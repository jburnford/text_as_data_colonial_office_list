Education

University courses in Theology, Arts, Science, Economic Studies and Education are provided at Fourah Bay College (University of Durham), Freetown. At the beginning of the 1958–59 session, 286 students were in attendance at these courses and, apart from scholarships for secondary education and teacher training, 114 scholarships were awarded, 58 for higher education, including study overseas. About 36 students were known to be studying in the U.S.A.

There are five Teacher Training Colleges and one Teacher Training Department in the territory; 604 students were in training in 1958.

There are seven Government secondary schools and 17 assisted secondary schools in which 5,452 pupils were enrolled in 1958. There are also two unassisted secondary schools which had 452 pupils. Two Government, 495 assisted and 25 unassisted primary schools had an enrolment of 69,276 pupils.

In 1958 there were two technical schools with 265 full-time and 872 part-time students, a vocational centre for girls with an enrolment of 68.
and 3 pre-training centres for unqualified teachers with 117 students.

Literacy campaigns were conducted in 1958 by the Protectorate Literature Bureau in 66 chiefdoms, and 546 reading certificates were issued. The Bureau reported that in ten years 45,200 people were estimated to have attained literacy in one vernacular, while 311,532 books in five vernaculars (Mende, Temne, Limba, Kono, and Koranko), had been sold, 144,943 of them within the last five years of the period.

The Extramural Department of Fourah Bay College, the British Council and the Social Development Department organized adult education courses in various subjects.

Recurrent expenditure on education was £1,619,956 in 1958. In 1935 total expenditure amounted to £38,518.
