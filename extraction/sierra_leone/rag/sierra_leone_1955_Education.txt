Education

University courses in Divinity, Arts, Commerce and Education are provided at Fourah Bay College (University of Durham), Freetown. At the beginning of 1953–54 session, 149 students were in attendance at these courses. Seventy scholarships for higher education were awarded in 1953 (30 for study overseas, 12 for degree courses at Fourah Bay College, and 28 for Higher School Certificate and General Certificate of Education courses at Fourah Bay College, Prince of Wales School and Bo Government School). Seventy-two Sierra Leone scholarship students and about 196 private students were known to be studying in the United Kingdom. About 45 private students were known to be studying in the U.S.A.

There are five Teacher Training Colleges and two Teacher Training Departments in the territory; 415 students, including 24 agricultural instructors, were in training in 1953.

There are two Government Secondary Grammar Schools, three Government Secondary Central Schools and nine assisted Secondary Schools in the territory; 3,703 students were enrolled in 1953. There are also two unassisted Secondary Schools, which had 306 pupils. Four Government, 257 assisted and 96 unassisted Primary Schools had an enrolment of 46,833 pupils.

Literacy campaigns were conducted by the Protectorate Literature Bureau in chiefdoms and 1,450 reading certificates were issued. It is claimed that between 25,000 and 30,000 people
have become literate in the vernacular, and 35,000 books in Mende, Kono, Temne and Kuranko were sold or distributed in 1953.

The Extramural Department of Fourah Bay College, the British Council and the Social Welfare Department organized adult education courses in various subjects.

Expenditure from all sources on education was approximately £622,504 in 1953 of which £218,231 was contributed from Colonial Development and Welfare funds. In 1935 expenditure amounted to £38,518.
